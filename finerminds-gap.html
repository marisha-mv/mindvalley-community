01 — The principles that make it work

What turns features into retention

Features alone don't retain people — how they're wired together does. These are the lessons from how the best engagement platforms (Strava, Duolingo) actually keep people coming back, each tied to the features below.

1 · The feed is member activity, not broadcast

The main content has to be what members do — their wins, progress and challenge updates — not brand posts. The daily tip is the nudge; the members are the feed. If the feed is mostly us talking, it's a newsletter in a community costume.

Delivered byLikes, comments & sharing wins (04) · Progress Cards (05) · Member-started challenges (02) · Testimonials (14)
Watch-out: don't let brand and AI posts dominate the feed.

2 · The social graph is the retention spine

Retention tracks how connected a member is, not how good the content is. The week-one job is getting each new member following a handful of people and into a pod — that's what makes leaving cost something.

Delivered byBuddy & friends (09) · Niche pods (01) · Velvet-rope invites (10) · "Members to Meet" (live today)
Watch-out: Buddy & friends sits at #9 — for retention it's closer to a spine than a nice-to-have.

3 · Engineer the week-one "aha"

The first sessions decide retention. Define one week-one behavioural milestone — posts a win, gets a few reactions, joins a pod within 7 days — and make onboarding drive it. "Feel seen" is a feeling; this is a number you can move.

The one gapThere's no activation / first-week onboarding flow in the list yet — the single feature I'd add. It routes a new member to their pod, their first win, and their first few connections inside the first session.

4 · The product does the work, not the manager

Engagement can't hinge on a heroic community manager posting all day. The system, the AI and the members should carry it, so the manager is leverage, not labour. Test: if the community goes quiet when the manager takes a week off, the system isn't doing enough.

Delivered byAI engagement & pings (03) · Spark + identity levels (07, 08) · Demand map (13)

5 · Protect the trust — cap the selling

The same trust that makes someone share a transformation is what you spend when you pitch them. Ring-fence the ratio of promotional beats to purely-for-them beats, or the feed goes quiet.

GovernsPersonal broadcast (11) · Affiliate marketplace (12) — use sparingly in the feed

6 · Velvet rope is acquisition, not retention

Invites get a spike; a sticky loop keeps people. Clubhouse is the warning, not the model — explosive invite growth, then collapse, because the core loop wasn't sticky. Pair the invite engine with the features that actually retain.

Delivered byVelvet-rope invites (10) for growth — Niche pods (01), Challenges (02) & Identity levels (08) for stickiness

7 · Replace the live-call heartbeat with cohort challenges

If live calls are cut or uncertain, time-boxed cohort challenges become the synchronous pulse — everyone starting the same thing the same week. It's the closest thing to a shared "we're all in this right now" moment.

Delivered byMember-started challenges (02), run on a seasonal / cohort cadence

03 — Features to build

What to build

The first four are the build-first set. The rest follow, grouped to flow. Each carries an impact tier and an indicative build effort for the tech team to validate.

ImpactHighestHigh
Effort (indicative)Light  Medium  Heavy
★ Build first
01

Niche pods — member-created, member-led

HighestMedium

Members can create and lead their own pods around a specific niche — an ecom pod, a runners pod — each its own thread. Includes small accountability pods where a handful of members keep each other going.

02

Member-started challenges

HighestMedium

Any member can start a challenge and bring others in to do it with them — quit smoking, a running streak — rallying their own people around a shared goal.

03

AI engagement, guidance & accountability

HighestHeavy

The AI engages with members directly — responds to them, and pings anyone who hasn't come back in a week to pull them in, covering the gaps while the community manager runs the calls. Pings are personalized: a post to read, a pod or challenge to join, a workshop to attend. It also guides and coaches each member using the authors' work, and runs a daily accountability and feedback loop — checking in on the practice, what they did and what happened.

04

Engagement — likes, comments & sharing wins

HighestLight

Members like, comment on, and share each other's progress. The dopamine of being seen is what brings people back to post again. Members can share their wins on the app, or publicly out to their other social.

Then
05

Progress Cards — the growth loop

HighMedium

When a member hits a milestone — finishes a quest, completes a challenge, hits a streak, or marks who they're becoming — the app auto-generates a beautiful, branded card from their real progress ("Day 30 of my 5am practice"), built to look right on an Instagram or Facebook story. They share it because it signals who they're becoming; followers see a branded card with a join link, and some come in. A challenge card links straight into the live challenge, so a friend joins inside the thing, with them — not just inspired. When someone joins through a card, the sharer earns Spark, and we can see which member drove the signup.

06

Monthly transformation recap

HighMedium

A personalized monthly recap of each member's progress — practices done, wins, who they're becoming — in the style of Spotify Wrapped, and built to share on social.

07

Spark points

HighMedium

Members earn points for consumption, engagement and sharing, and redeem them later.

08

Identity levels — status tiers

HighMedium

Status tiers — Bronze, Silver, Gold — that members climb as they earn Spark points, the way loyalty brands work. Each tier unlocks more features and perks (Gold might get event-pass discounts or free passes), so members have a status to chase and a reason to stay active.

09

Buddy & friends

HighMedium

Members can find which of their friends are already on the app and add them, see what their friends are doing — the challenges they're in, what they're attending — and invite or share with friends to join what they're doing or attending.

10

Velvet-rope entry & the invite engine

HighMedium

The community is free, but you can't just walk in — you apply, or you're invited. Borrowing Clubhouse's growth engine: every person who joins gets 3 invites to bring in or recommend three people. Anyone they invite who isn't already a member lands in the free founder community. Scarcity and word of mouth scale it; capped invites plus the application keep the bar high, so it stays a room worth being in — the way Raya and Clubhouse grew.

11

Personal broadcast

HighLight–Med

The host sends one message to many, but each lands looking like a private 1:1 DM — ideal for inviting members to classes and summits without it feeling like a blast.

12

Affiliate marketplace

HighHeavy

A marketplace where members and experts can list their own products and services to the community. Mindvalley takes a 30% cut of every sale.

13

Demand map

HighMedium

A living map of the keywords, questions and topics members raise — and it keeps growing — so we always know what people are interested in and what challenges they're facing.

14

Testimonials — member-added & host-requested

HighMedium

Members can add testimonials directly from the community, and the host can request a testimonial from a member or a whole cohort. Tracked by membership tenure (month 1–5).
